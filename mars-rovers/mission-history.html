The Legacy of Martian Exploration

Each mission, from the original Mars Exploration Rovers (MER) to the cutting-edge Mars 2020 mission, has built upon the discoveries and lessons learned from its predecessors, fundamentally changing our perception of Mars from a cold, dead world to a dynamic planet that once held promise for life.

Spirit and Opportunity (MER Program)

Launched in 2003, the twin rovers Spirit and Opportunity were designed for a 90-day mission. They far exceeded expectations, providing evidence of past liquid water on Mars. Opportunity operated for almost 15 years, covering over 45 kilometers and proving the exceptional longevity of solar-powered exploration on the Martian surface. Spirit also operated for over six years before getting stuck in soft sand. Their work set the stage for the larger, nuclear-powered rovers to follow.

Curiosity: The Search for Habitability

The Curiosity rover, part of the Mars Science Laboratory (MSL) mission, marked a major leap forward in size and sophistication. Landing in 2012, its goal was to assess the past and present habitability of the planet, particularly within Gale Crater. Curiosity confirmed that Mars once had flowing water and the necessary chemical building blocks to support microbial life, fulfilling the primary objective of the mission and paving the way for the direct search for biosignatures.

Detailed Mission Timeline

1975: Viking Program Launch

NASA launches the Viking 1 and 2 missions, which successfully placed two orbiters and two landers on Mars, providing the first color images from the surface and conducting complex biology experiments.

1997: Sojourner Rover (Pathfinder Mission)

The Mars Pathfinder mission successfully deploys Sojourner, the first wheeled rover to operate on the surface of Mars, proving the concept of mobile exploration.

2003: Spirit and Opportunity Launch

NASA launches the two Mars Exploration Rovers, Spirit and Opportunity.

2004: MER Landings

Spirit lands in Gusev Crater (January 3); Opportunity lands in Meridiani Planum (January 24).

2010: Spirit Mission Ends

Spirit becomes stuck in soft sand and ceases communications the following year.

2011: Curiosity Launch

The Mars Science Laboratory (MSL) mission launches carrying the Curiosity rover.

2012: Curiosity Landing (Gale Crater)

Curiosity successfully lands and begins its mission to assess past habitability.

2018: Opportunity Mission Ends

After a planet-wide dust storm, NASA declares the Opportunity mission complete.

2020: Perseverance Launch

The Mars 2020 mission launches, carrying Perseverance and the Ingenuity helicopter.

2021: Perseverance Landing (Jezero Crater)

Perseverance lands and begins collecting rock samples.

2021: Ingenuity's First Flight

Ingenuity achieves the first powered, controlled flight on another planet.
